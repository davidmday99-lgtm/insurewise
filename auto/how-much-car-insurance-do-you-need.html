How Much Car Insurance Do You Actually Need?

Published March 14, 2026 · 6 min read

Here's a scary fact: if you're carrying only your state's minimum coverage and you cause a serious accident, you could be personally on the hook for tens — or even hundreds — of thousands of dollars. State minimums keep you legal. They don't keep you protected.

So how much do you actually need? Let's break it down without the insurance jargon.

The Coverage Levels, Explained Simply

When you see coverage listed as 100/300/100, here's what those numbers mean:

  • First number (100): $100,000 per person for bodily injury you cause
  • Second number (300): $300,000 total per accident for bodily injury
  • Third number (100): $100,000 for property damage you cause

State Minimums vs. Recommended Coverage

Coverage Level Example Who It's For
State Minimum 25/50/25 Barely legal. Only if you have zero assets to protect.
Better 50/100/50 Decent starting point for budget-conscious drivers.
Recommended 100/300/100 Sweet spot for most drivers. Affordable protection.
Maximum 250/500/250 If you own a home, have savings, or higher income.

A Real Example

Scenario: You rear-end someone on the highway. Two people in the other car are injured. Medical bills total $180,000. Their car is $45,000 in damage.

With 25/50/25 coverage: Your insurance pays $50,000 max for injuries (split between two people) and $25,000 for the car. You're personally responsible for the remaining $150,000.

With 100/300/100 coverage: Your insurance covers all of it. You pay nothing out of pocket.

The Cost Difference Is Smaller Than You Think

Moving from state minimums to 100/300/100 typically costs $20-40 more per month. That's less than a dollar a day to avoid a potential six-figure lawsuit.

Other Coverage Worth Considering

  • Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM): ~13% of drivers have no insurance. This covers you when they hit you. Match it to your liability limits.
  • Collision & Comprehensive: If your car is worth more than $4,000-5,000, these are usually worth having. Covers your own car in accidents, theft, weather, etc.
  • Medical Payments (MedPay): Covers your medical bills regardless of fault. Usually cheap ($5-15/month) and worth adding.

Bottom Line

If you own a home, have savings, or earn a decent income, carry at least 100/300/100. If you have significant assets (home equity, investments, high income), consider 250/500/250 or an umbrella policy.

State minimums exist to keep you legal. Smart coverage exists to keep you protected. The difference is usually less than your monthly streaming subscriptions.
