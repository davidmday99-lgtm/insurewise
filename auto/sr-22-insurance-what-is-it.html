SR-22 Insurance: What It Is, How Much It Costs, and How to Get It

Published March 16, 2026 · 7 min read

If a court or your state DMV has told you that you need an SR-22, you're probably wondering what that means and how much it's going to cost you. Here's the complete guide — no legal jargon, just the facts.

What Is an SR-22?

An SR-22 is not insurance. It's a certificate of financial responsibility that your insurance company files with your state to prove you're carrying the minimum required auto insurance coverage.

Think of it as your insurance company telling the state: "Yes, this person is insured. We'll notify you if their policy lapses."

The filing itself typically costs $15-50 as a one-time fee. The real cost comes from what the SR-22 represents — you've had a serious driving incident, and your insurance rates are going up because of it.

When Do You Need an SR-22?

You'll typically need an SR-22 if you've been convicted of or involved in any of the following:

  • DUI or DWI conviction — the most common reason by far
  • Reckless driving
  • Driving without insurance
  • At-fault accident while uninsured
  • Multiple traffic violations in a short period (3+ in 12 months)
  • License suspension or revocation
  • Filing for hardship license after suspension
⚠️ Important: Not every state uses SR-22s. Virginia, Florida, and Delaware use similar but different forms. Some states (like New York and North Carolina) don't require SR-22 filings at all.

How Much Does an SR-22 Cost?

There are two costs you need to understand:

1. The filing fee: $15-50

This is the one-time fee your insurance company charges to file the SR-22 with the state. It's trivial.

2. The increased premium: $500-3,000+/year

This is where it hurts. Because an SR-22 means you've had a serious violation, your insurance rates go up significantly. Here's what to expect:

Reason for SR-22Avg. Annual IncreaseNew Avg. Annual Rate
DUI/DWI+$2,000-4,000$3,500-6,000
Reckless driving+$1,000-2,000$2,500-4,000
Driving uninsured+$800-1,500$2,000-3,500
Multiple violations+$600-1,200$1,800-3,000

These increases typically last for 3 years — the standard SR-22 requirement period. After that, your rates should start to come back down.

How Long Do You Need an SR-22?

In most states, you need to maintain an SR-22 for 3 years. However, it varies:

  • 3 years: Most states (California, Texas, Florida, Illinois, etc.)
  • 2 years: Some states for minor violations
  • 5 years: Some states for serious/repeat offenses

If your insurance lapses during the SR-22 period, your insurer is required to notify the state. This can result in license suspension and restarting the clock.

Which Insurance Companies Offer SR-22s?

Most major insurers will file an SR-22 for you. However, some companies specialize in high-risk drivers:

  • Progressive — best for high-risk/SR-22 drivers, competitive rates
  • GEICO — files SR-22s, competitive for most driver profiles
  • State Farm — may not drop you after a first DUI
  • National General — specializes in non-standard (high-risk) insurance
  • Dairyland — known for SR-22 filings and high-risk coverage
  • The General — targets high-risk drivers specifically
💡 Pro tip: If your current insurer drops you after a violation, don't panic. Companies like Progressive and National General specialize in exactly this situation. Shop around — even high-risk rates vary by $1,000+/year between companies.

How to Get an SR-22 (Step by Step)

  1. Get car insurance. You need an active policy before the SR-22 can be filed. If your current insurer won't cover you, shop for high-risk insurers (Progressive, National General, Dairyland).
  2. Ask your insurer to file the SR-22. Tell them you need an SR-22 filing. They'll handle the paperwork with your state DMV.
  3. Pay the filing fee. Usually $15-50, added to your first premium payment.
  4. Wait for confirmation. The filing typically processes in 1-3 business days. Some states are same-day.
  5. Don't let your policy lapse. If your insurance lapses, the insurer notifies the state immediately, and your license can be suspended.

SR-22 vs. FR-44: What's the Difference?

Florida and Virginia use an FR-44 instead of (or in addition to) an SR-22 for DUI convictions. The key difference:

  • SR-22: Proves you carry state minimum liability coverage
  • FR-44: Requires higher liability limits — typically 100/300/50 (much more than minimums)

If you need an FR-44, expect your premium to be even higher because you're required to carry more coverage.

Can You Remove an SR-22 Early?

Generally, no. The requirement period is set by the court or DMV and isn't negotiable. However:

  • Some states allow early removal for good behavior (rare)
  • Moving to a state that doesn't require SR-22s doesn't remove the requirement in your original state
  • Once the period is over, you can ask your insurer to remove the filing and potentially lower your rate

The Bottom Line

An SR-22 is annoying and expensive, but it's manageable. The filing fee is trivial — the real cost is the premium increase, which averages $1,500-3,000/year extra. Shop around aggressively (especially Progressive and high-risk specialists), maintain continuous coverage, and after 3 years, your rates will improve significantly.
