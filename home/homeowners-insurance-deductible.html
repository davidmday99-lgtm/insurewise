Homeowners Insurance Deductible: $500, $1,000, or $2,500?

Published March 14, 2026 · 5 min read

Your deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before insurance kicks in. Pick too low and you overpay in premiums. Pick too high and a small claim wipes out your savings. Here's how to find the sweet spot.

Quick Comparison

DeductibleAvg. Premium SavingsBest For
$500BaselineTight budgets, older homes, frequent small claims risk
$1,00010-15% lessMost homeowners. Good balance of savings and risk.
$2,50020-30% lessEmergency fund holders, newer homes, low-risk areas
$5,000+30-40% lessHigh net worth, very low-risk homes, large savings

The 6-Month Rule

Simple formula: Choose the highest deductible you could comfortably pay from savings without financial stress. If you have $5,000 in emergency savings, a $2,500 deductible is fine. If you have $1,000, stick with $1,000.

When a Higher Deductible Saves You Money

On a typical $1,800/year homeowners policy:

  • $500 → $1,000 deductible: Save ~$180-270/year (pays for itself in 2 years)
  • $1,000 → $2,500 deductible: Save ~$200-360/year
  • $2,500 → $5,000 deductible: Save ~$150-250/year (diminishing returns)

The key insight: if you don't file a claim for 3-4 years, the higher deductible saves you money even without a claim.

Special Deductibles to Know About

Your policy likely has separate deductibles for:

  • Hurricane/Wind: Often 1-5% of your dwelling coverage (on a $300K home: $3,000-$15,000)
  • Flood: Completely separate policy with its own deductible ($1,000-$10,000)
  • Earthquake: Separate policy, typically 10-20% of dwelling coverage

Bottom Line

For most homeowners, $1,000-$2,500 is the sweet spot. Low enough that you can cover it, high enough that your premiums are reasonable. The money you save on premiums over 3-4 years typically exceeds the extra you'd pay in a deductible if something happens.
