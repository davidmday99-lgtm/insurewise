What Does Homeowners Insurance Actually Cover?

Published March 16, 2026 · 9 min read

Your homeowners policy is probably 30+ pages of fine print. Here's what it actually covers — and more importantly, what it doesn't — in plain English.

The 6 Standard Coverages

Every standard homeowners policy (called an HO-3) includes these six types of coverage:

1. Dwelling Coverage (Coverage A)

This pays to rebuild or repair your home's structure if it's damaged by a covered event. This includes:

  • The house itself (walls, roof, foundation)
  • Built-in appliances
  • Attached structures (garage, deck)

How much you need: Enough to completely rebuild your home at current construction costs — NOT its market value. A $400,000 home might cost $250,000 to rebuild (land isn't included) or $500,000 (if construction costs are high in your area).

2. Other Structures (Coverage B)

Covers structures that aren't attached to your house:

  • Detached garage
  • Shed
  • Fence
  • Swimming pool
  • Guest house

Typical limit: 10% of your dwelling coverage. If your dwelling coverage is $300K, other structures are covered up to $30K.

3. Personal Property (Coverage C)

Your stuff — furniture, clothes, electronics, appliances, everything you own. This covers damage or theft, even if it happens outside your home (your laptop stolen from your car is covered).

Typical limit: 50-70% of dwelling coverage. $300K dwelling = $150-210K in personal property coverage.

💡 Important: Most policies cover personal property at "actual cash value" (depreciated value). If your 5-year-old TV is destroyed, they'll pay what a 5-year-old TV is worth — not what a new one costs. Upgrade to "replacement cost" coverage for a few dollars more per month.

4. Loss of Use / Additional Living Expenses (Coverage D)

If your home is uninhabitable after a covered event, this pays for:

  • Hotel or temporary rental
  • Restaurant meals (beyond your normal food budget)
  • Laundry, storage, and other extra costs

Typical limit: 20-30% of dwelling coverage. Covers you for 12-24 months in most policies.

5. Personal Liability (Coverage E)

If someone sues you for injury or property damage that happens on your property (or caused by you anywhere), this covers:

  • Legal defense costs
  • Settlements or judgments
  • Medical bills for injured guests

Typical limit: $100,000-$500,000. Most experts recommend at least $300K. If you have significant assets, consider an umbrella policy for additional coverage.

6. Medical Payments (Coverage F)

Pays medical bills for people injured on your property, regardless of fault. This is no-fault coverage — it pays out even if the injury was the guest's fault.

Typical limit: $1,000-$5,000. This is separate from liability and much smaller.

What's Covered: Named Perils vs. Open Perils

Standard HO-3 policies cover:

  • Dwelling: "Open perils" (all-risks) — everything is covered except what's specifically excluded
  • Personal property: "Named perils" — only the 16 listed events are covered

The 16 named perils for personal property:

  1. Fire and lightning
  2. Windstorm and hail
  3. Explosion
  4. Riot and civil commotion
  5. Damage from aircraft
  6. Damage from vehicles
  7. Smoke damage
  8. Vandalism
  9. Theft
  10. Volcanic eruption
  11. Falling objects
  12. Ice, snow, and sleet weight
  13. Water overflow/leak from plumbing
  14. Accidental electrical discharge
  15. Sudden tearing of HVAC/water heating
  16. Freezing of pipes

What's NOT Covered

This is where people get surprised. Standard homeowners policies do NOT cover:

Floods

Requires a separate flood insurance policy (through FEMA's NFIP or private flood insurers). If you're in a flood zone, your mortgage lender will require this.

Earthquakes

Requires a separate earthquake policy or endorsement. Important in California, the Pacific Northwest, and other seismically active areas.

Sewer/Drain Backup

Water backing up from sewers or drains is excluded. You can add this as an endorsement for $40-150/year — highly recommended.

Maintenance Issues

Wear and tear, mold, pest damage (termites, rodents), and gradual deterioration are never covered. Insurance covers sudden events, not neglect.

Home Business Equipment

Business equipment in your home is typically limited to $2,500. If you work from home with expensive equipment, get a business endorsement or separate policy.

Expensive Valuables

Jewelry, art, collectibles, and firearms are usually capped at $1,500-$2,500 total. For anything valuable, schedule it separately (called a "rider" or "floater").

⚠️ Common surprise: Water damage is the #1 source of denied claims. "Sudden and accidental" water damage (burst pipe) IS covered. Gradual leaks, flooding, and sewer backups are NOT covered unless you add endorsements or separate policies.

How Much Coverage Do You Actually Need?

Dwelling: Enough to rebuild

Get a rebuild estimate from a local contractor or use your insurer's replacement cost calculator. Update this every 3-5 years as construction costs rise.

Personal property: Do a quick inventory

Walk through your home and mentally add up your stuff. Most people have $50,000-$150,000 in personal property. Take photos/video of everything for claims.

Liability: At least $300K

$100K is the minimum, but $300K-$500K is smarter. If your net worth exceeds $500K, add an umbrella policy ($1M+ in additional liability for $200-400/year).

Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value

This is one of the most important choices in your policy:

TypeWhat You GetExample (5-year-old roof)
Actual Cash ValueDepreciated value of damaged itemPays $8,000 (roof worth $8K after 5 years of depreciation)
Replacement CostCost to replace with new equivalentPays $15,000 (cost of a new roof today)

Replacement cost coverage costs more but is almost always worth it. Without it, a major claim could leave you paying tens of thousands out of pocket.

The Bottom Line

Standard homeowners insurance covers more than most people think — but it also has significant gaps (flood, earthquake, maintenance, sewer backup). Review your policy, make sure dwelling coverage is enough to rebuild, add a sewer backup endorsement, and upgrade to replacement cost coverage. Total cost for these improvements: usually $100-300/year extra. Peace of mind: priceless.
