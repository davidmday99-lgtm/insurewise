HSA vs FSA: What's the Difference?

Published March 16, 2026 · 7 min read

Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) and Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs) both let you pay for medical expenses with pre-tax dollars. But they work very differently — and choosing the wrong one (or missing out on either) could cost you thousands.

What Is an HSA?

A Health Savings Account is a personal savings account that you own, specifically for medical expenses. It comes with a triple tax advantage:

  1. Tax-free contributions — money goes in before taxes
  2. Tax-free growth — invest the balance, gains aren't taxed
  3. Tax-free withdrawals — spend on qualified medical expenses, no tax ever

There's no other account in the US tax code with this combination. Not 401(k)s, not IRAs, not Roth accounts. HSAs are the most tax-advantaged account that exists.

HSA requirements

  • You must be enrolled in a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)
  • 2026 minimum deductible: $1,650 (individual) / $3,300 (family)
  • 2026 max out-of-pocket: $8,300 (individual) / $16,600 (family)
  • You can't be enrolled in Medicare or claimed as a dependent

What Is an FSA?

A Flexible Spending Account is an employer-provided account for medical expenses. You elect how much to contribute during open enrollment, and it's deducted from your paycheck pre-tax throughout the year.

The critical limitation: use it or lose it. If you don't spend the money by the end of the plan year (or grace period), you forfeit it. Some plans allow you to roll over up to $640 — but anything above that is gone.

FSA types

  • Healthcare FSA: For medical, dental, and vision expenses (the most common type)
  • Dependent Care FSA: For childcare and elder care expenses (separate $5,000 limit)
  • Limited Purpose FSA: Only dental and vision — can be paired with an HSA

HSA vs FSA: Which Should You Choose?

Choose HSA if:

  • You have a high-deductible health plan (HDHP)
  • You want to invest for the long term (HSA is a stealth retirement account)
  • You rarely go to the doctor and want to build up savings
  • You want the money to roll over indefinitely
  • You might change jobs and want to keep your account

Choose FSA if:

  • You don't have an HDHP (can't get an HSA)
  • You have predictable medical expenses (regular prescriptions, ongoing treatment)
  • Your employer contributes to the FSA (free money)
  • You want to spend the money in the same year
💡 Best strategy: If you have an HDHP, max out your HSA. Don't spend it on current medical expenses if you can afford to pay out of pocket. Instead, invest the HSA and let it grow tax-free for decades. Pay medical expenses out of pocket, save receipts, and reimburse yourself from the HSA years later — completely tax-free. It's like a supercharged Roth IRA for medical expenses.

Can You Have Both an HSA and FSA?

Generally, no. Having both a general-purpose Healthcare FSA and an HSA disqualifies you from HSA contributions.

However, there's one exception: a Limited Purpose FSA (LPFSA). This type only covers dental and vision expenses and can be used alongside an HSA. If your employer offers one, it's a great combo — use the LPFSA for dental/vision, save the HSA for investment growth.

What Counts as a Qualified Medical Expense?

Both HSAs and FSAs can pay for:

  • Doctor visit copays and coinsurance
  • Prescription medications
  • Dental work (cleanings, fillings, braces, crowns)
  • Vision (exams, glasses, contacts, LASIK)
  • Mental health therapy
  • Chiropractic care
  • Medical equipment (crutches, blood pressure monitors)
  • Over-the-counter medications (Tylenol, allergy meds, etc.)
  • Menstrual care products
  • First aid supplies

The HSA Retirement Hack

Here's something most people don't know: after age 65, you can withdraw HSA funds for any purpose. If used for non-medical expenses, you'll pay income tax (like a traditional IRA) but no penalty. If used for medical expenses, it's completely tax-free — even in retirement.

This makes the HSA a uniquely flexible retirement tool:

  • Before 65: Use for medical expenses (tax-free)
  • After 65: Use for anything (taxed like IRA) or medical (still tax-free)
  • Medicare premiums in retirement are a qualified expense

A couple that maxes out their family HSA for 20 years and invests it could accumulate $400,000+ tax-free — all from medical expense savings.

⚠️ Common FSA mistake: The #1 error with FSAs is over-contributing and losing money at year-end. Be conservative with your election. If you spend $1,200/year on medical expenses, elect $1,400 — not $3,000. You can always pay out of pocket for small expenses; you can never recover forfeited FSA money.

The Bottom Line

If you have an HDHP, an HSA is one of the best financial tools available — the triple tax advantage is unmatched. Max it out, invest it, and let it grow. If you don't have an HDHP, an FSA is still worth using for the tax savings on predictable medical expenses — just be careful not to over-contribute.
